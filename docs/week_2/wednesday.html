Supporting your Design with Research

5:15 - 6:00

It is common for questions to naturally emerge in the course of creative projects.

  • Will people understand what I’m doing?
  • Has anyone done this before?
  • How do I make this work?
  • My project is about to X. What is X?
  • What do people think about X?
  • Does anyone actually want this?
  • How much will it cost?

Ideas for creative projects often emerge from insights gleaned from research.

Research is an necessary tool for designers who wish to make designs founded on understanding.

Today is just practice to introduce these tools, you should use these tools as you work through this—and every—project.

These are tools, not steps.

Choosing your Research Topic

Domains

Domains are areas of study in which a particular topic or project sits. Identifying the domains in which your project exists is important to understanding the relevant context for you work.

What domains were relevant to your 7-in-7 projects?

Domain Mapping and Reframing

Individual, 10 minutes

Use mapping to explore the relationships between related ideas.

Begin with a clean sheet of paper.

  1. Choose one of your 7-in-7 responses that you feel has potential for further development.
  2. Write down the primary domains associated with your topic in spaced out bubbles.
  3. Brainstorm concepts suggested by each domain, adding them to your map.
  4. Connect related concepts.
  5. Look for interesting overlaps, contrasts, and tensions; mark them on your map.
  6. Write down research topics suggested by these relationships.

Broad Topics + Focused Topics

Broad topics are easier to find sources for, harder to master, and boring.

Focused topics are harder to find sources for, easier to master, and interesting.

Exploring Topic Focus

Individual, 5 minutes

Consider the scope of your research topic. Is it too broad? Is it to narrow?

Begin with a clean sheet of paper.

  1. Choose a topic suggested by your domain mapping exercise, write that topic in the middle of your paper.
  2. At the top of the page write a very broad phrasing of your topic.
  3. At the bottom of the page write a very narrowly focused version of your topic.
  4. Add a middle-broad topic.
  5. Add a middle-focused topic.

A Research Statement

Individual, 5 minutes

I am studying ____1_____
because I want to find out _____2_____
in order to | to support making _____3______.

  1. Start by simply stating your focused research topic.
  2. Explain what you do not know.
  3. Explain why it is important. What happens if you don’t find an answer.

Presenting your Findings

6:15—6:30

You will present your findings with a 3-4 minute slide presentation.

0. Plan

Presenting in a short time requires planning and practice. Take the time to identify the most important information and rehearse to make sure you have time to present everything you plan to.

Almost all of your information should be conveyed by what you say, use slides sparingly to support what you say.

Don’t put any text on your slides you don’t expect us to read. Make sure you give us enough quiet time to read it.

1. Hook 'em

Before you get to the primary point, give us a reason to be interested.

A personal, relatable anecdote or a specific, thought-provoking question is often an effective opener.

2. Tell 'em What You’ll Tell 'em

Briefly tell us what your presentation is about, and what we will know at the end. This helps us organize the information in our heads as you deliver it. For this presentation you might do this using the research statement template. In a presentation about your project you might show your finished project first.

3. Tell 'em

Convey your research findings. Specifically connect everything you tell us to the larger context and your main point.

4. Tell 'em what you told 'em

Bring your presentation to a clear conclusion.
